The gap nobody caught in third grade

By fifth grade, reading instruction mostly stops. Students are expected to read to learn — and the ones still missing foundational skills quietly fall further behind every year. You may recognise some of this:

  • They guess at longer words instead of breaking them apart.
  • They read word-by-word, flat and slow, with no expression.
  • They finish a page and can’t tell you what happened in it.
  • They’ve started avoiding reading altogether — and calling themselves “bad at it.”
  • Every intervention workbook you’ve found is written for a six-year-old.

None of that means your student can’t read. It means nobody ever taught them the system underneath the words.

You don’t need a reading degree to teach this

Most workbooks hand you a page and wish you luck. Every instructional page in the Playbook carries a Coach’s Script — the actual words to say to introduce the skill — and a Know the Play box explaining the concept in plain language before your student ever picks up a pencil.

The coaching method is simple enough to hold in your head: Pause, Prompt, Praise. Wait. Give the smallest hint that works. Name what they did right. Fifteen minutes a day, four steps — warm-up, teach, practise, reflect.

The student side speaks in a different voice. They’re the All-Star. You’re the coach. For a twelve-year-old who has spent years being remediated, that framing does real work.

Is this the right book for your student?

It won’t suit everyone, and I’d rather tell you now.

A good fit if…

  • Your student is in grades 5–8 and reading below grade level.
  • They stumble on long words and read without expression.
  • You want structured-literacy instruction you can actually deliver at the kitchen table.
  • You’re homeschooling, tutoring, or running Tier 2 / Tier 3 intervention.
  • You have fifteen minutes a day and a printer.

Probably not if…

  • Your child is a beginning reader still learning letter sounds — this starts past that.
  • You want a self-paced app. This is paper, and it needs an adult beside it.
  • You’re looking for a formal diagnosis. A dyslexia evaluation is a job for a specialist.
  • You want something done to your student rather than with them.

Who wrote this

I’m David Lane. I’ve spent more than twenty years in classrooms, hold a Master’s in Educational Leadership and TESOL certification, and I homeschool my own teenager — which is where most of this book actually comes from.

I built the Playbook because I kept looking for an intervention resource for older students and kept finding material designed for kindergarteners. A struggling seventh grader knows exactly what a cartoon worksheet means. It means you think I’m a baby. They shut down, and the reading gap gets wider.

So this one is built on structured-literacy principles — the same systematic phonics, fluency, and comprehension progression the science of reading points to — and dressed as a sports playbook, because a kid who has failed at reading can still be an athlete learning a system.
